Category Theory: An Introduction

Mar 4 2020 Mathematics 17 minutes read (About 2510 words)

“The language of categories is affectionately known as “abstract nonsense,” so named by Norman Steenrod. This term is essentially accurate and not necessarily derogatory: categories refer to “nonsense” in the sense that they are all about the “structure,” and not about the “meaning,” of what they represent.”

—Paolo Aluffi, Algebra: Chapter 0

Introduced in the 1940s by Mac Lane and Eilenberg in a paper titled “General Theory of Natural Equivalences”, Category theory is a branch of mathematics that aims to unify various mathematical structures from different fields and provide generalized concepts and theorems, and is thus known for its abstractness and generality. In this post, I am going to explain two important concepts in category theory: the Yoneda Lemma and the universal property. I am assuming that the reader understands introductory abstract algebra (knows the definition of groups, rings, fields, vector spaces and modules) and perferably some topology. Throughout this post, we accept the Zermelo-Fraenkel axioms as our foundations, and use \(\mathcal{U}\) to refer to the universe of all sets. However, it should also be noted that there are other books relating category theory where the universe \(\mathcal{U}\) in discussion is not the universe of all set but rather something else such as the Grothendieck Universe, which is not a topic of discussion in this post.

The Smith Determinant

Jul 12 2019 Mathematics a minute read (About 213 words)

Here’s a curious identity I ran across on AoPS: the determinant of an \(n\times n\) matrix where the \((i,j)\) entries is \(\gcd(i,j)\) happends to be the product of the Euler totient values from \(1\) to \(n\).

Theorem (Smith’s Determinant).
$$
\det\begin{pmatrix}
\gcd(1,1)&\gcd(1,2)&\cdots&\gcd(1,n)\\
\gcd(2,1)&\gcd(2,2)&\cdots&\gcd(2,n)\\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots &\vdots\\
\gcd(n,1)&\gcd(n,2)&\cdots&\gcd(n,n)
\end{pmatrix}=\prod_{m=1}^n\varphi(m)
$$where \(\varphi(\cdot)\) is the Euler totient function.

The proof of this result is surprisingly simple: to describe it briefly, multiply the first row by \(-1\) and add it to the \(2,3,4,…,n\) rows, multiply the second row by \(-1\) and add it to the \(2,4,6,…\) rows, multiply the third row by \(-1\) and add it to the \(3,6,9,…\) rows, and so on. Then use of the identity \(\sum_{d\mid n}\varphi(d)=n\) to simplify. Another method is to use of UD decomposition, decomposing the original matrix into
$$
\begin{pmatrix}
\varphi(1)&0&0&0&0&\cdots\\
\varphi(1)&\varphi(2)&0&0&0&\cdots\\
\varphi(1)&0&\varphi(3)&0&0&\cdots\\
\varphi(1)&\varphi(2)&0&\varphi(4)&0&\cdots\\
\varphi(1)&0&0&0&\varphi(5)&\cdots\\
\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots\\
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
1&1&1&1&1&\cdots\\
0&1&0&1&0&\cdots\\
0&0&1&0&0&\cdots\\
0&0&0&1&0&\cdots\\
0&0&0&0&1&\cdots\\
\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots\\
\end{pmatrix}
$$And hence by the multiplicative property of determinants, we have our desired result.

Burnside's Lemma and Polya Enumeration Theorem

Jul 11 2019 Mathematics 5 minutes read (About 738 words)

Burnside’s lemma is a lemma of elementary group theory that is very useful in counting, and often seen as an “overkill” trick in math competitions. Intuitively, Burnside’s lemma counts the number of arrangements or permutations of an object if some of its symmetries are considered identical. For example,

Example 1.

How many distinct colorings can be obtained with \(6\) different colors to color the faces of a cube? Two configurations are considered identical if one can be obtained from the other through rotation.

Solution

There are \(30\) distinct colorings. Consider the group of rotations of a cube \(G\) acting on \(\mathrm{S}_6\), there are \(3\times 3=9\) rotations about opposite faces, \(6\times 1 = 6\) rotations about opposite edges, and \(4\times 2=8\) rotations about opposite vertices. Together with the identity rotation we have \(|G|=9+6+8+1=24\) (in fact, \(G\) is isomorphic to \(\mathrm{S}_4 \)). For \(g=e\), we have \(X^g=X\), and for \(g\ne e\), we have \(X^g=\varnothing\) since all colors on the faces are different. Hence the number of orbits is
$$
\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g\in G}|X^g|=\frac{|X|}{|G|}=\frac{6!}{24}=30
$$which means that there are \(30\) distinct colorings.

To approach such problem, we start with the definitions of orbits, stabilizers, and fixed points in group actions.

Definition 1.

Let \(G\) be a group that acts on \(X\), the orbit of a point \( x\in X\) is defined as \( G\cdot x=\{g\cdot x\mid g\in G\} \), the stabilizer of a point \( x\in X\) is defined as \( G_x =\{g\in G\mid g\cdot x=x\} \), and the fixed points of \(g\in G\) in \(X\) is the set \( X^g=\{x\in X\mid g\cdot x=x\} \).

Note that for \(x,y\in X\), either the orbits \(G\cdot x=G\cdot y\) or \((G\cdot x)\cap (G\cdot y)=\varnothing \). This is an easy result since if \(a\in (G\cdot x)\cap (G\cdot y)\) then \(a=g_1\cdot x=g_2\cdot y\), for some \(g_1,g_2\in G\), so \(x=g_1^{-1}g_2\cdot y\) and \(y=g_2^{-1}g_1\cdot x\), hence \((G\cdot x)\subseteq (G\cdot y)\) and \((G\cdot y)\subseteq (G\cdot x)\). Also, consider the map \(\varphi:G/G_x\rightarrow G\cdot x\) defined by \(\varphi(gG_x)=g\cdot x\). We can easily see that \(\varphi\) is an isomorphism, which gives us \(|G\cdot x|=\left[G:G_x\right]\), also known as the orbit-stabilizer theorem.

Quadratic Reciprocity Times Three

Feb 21 2019 Mathematics 3 minutes read (About 412 words)

Besides the Pythagorean theorem and the fundamental theorem of algebra, the law of Quadratic Reciprocity is probably the most oftenly proved theorem in math. In fact, on this website you can find 246 different proofs (collected by the mathematician Franz Lemmermeyer) of the theorem. In this article, I will discuss a few of them that I thought are the best ones. First, we define what a quadratic residue is.

Definition. For an odd prime $p$ and an integer $a\in\mathbf{Z}$ coprime to $p$, if there exists $n\in\mathbf{Z}$ such that $ a\equiv n^2\pmod{p}$ then we say that $a$ is a quadratic residue modulo $p$ (a quadratic non-residue otherwise).

It is easy to see that in $\mathbf{Z}/p\mathbf{Z}$ there are exactly $\frac{p-1}{2}$ quadratic residues, namely, $1^2,2^2,\dots,\left(\frac{p-1}{2}\right)^2$. To see this, note that if $i^2\equiv j^2\pmod{p}$ for $1\le i,j\le \frac{p-1}{2}$, then $p\mid (i+j)(i-j)$ but $2\le i+j\le p-1$ so $i\equiv j\pmod{p}$. Next we introduce the Legendre symbol, with which we can write the law of quadratic reciprocity concisely.

Definition. For an odd prime $p$ and $a\in \mathbf{Z}$
$$
\left(\dfrac{a}{p}\right):=
\left\{\begin{matrix}
1&a\mathrm{\; is\; a\; quadratic\; residue\; modulo\;}p\\
-1&a\mathrm{\; is\; a\; quadratic\; nonresidue\; modulo\;}p\\
0&a\equiv 0\pmod{p}\\
\end{matrix}\right.
$$
Theorem (Quadratic Reciprocity). If $p,q$ are two distinct odd primes, then
$$
\left(\dfrac{p}{q}\right)\left(\dfrac{q}{p}\right)=(-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}\cdot \frac{q-1}{2}}
$$
To prove this, we first introduce a useful lemma by Euler. By Fermat’s little theorem, in $(\mathbf{Z}/p\mathbf{Z})[x]$, the polynomial $x^{p-1}-1=(x^{\frac{p-1}{2}}+1)(x^{\frac{p-1}{2}}-1)$ takes on roots over all nonzero residues. And, since if $a\equiv n^2\pmod{p}$ is a quadratic residue, then $a^{\frac{p-1}{2}}\equiv n^{p-1}\equiv 1\pmod{p}$, the quadratic residues are precisely roots of ​$x^{\frac{p-1}{2}}-1$, in other words,

Lemma (Euler’s Criterion). For an odd prime $p$ and $a\in \mathbf{Z}$
$$
\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)=a^{\frac{p-1}{2}}\pmod{p}
$$
This immediately gives the corollary that the Legendre symbol is a completely multiplicative function, that is $\left(\frac{ab}{p}\right)=\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)\left(\frac{b}{p}\right)$ for $a,b\in\mathbf{Z}$ and odd prime $p$.
