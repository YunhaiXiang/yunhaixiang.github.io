Basic Notions in Category Theory with Haskell

Mar 4 2020 Mathematics 10 minutes read (About 1518 words)

“The language of categories is affectionately known as “abstract nonsense,” so named by Norman Steenrod. This term is essentially accurate and not necessarily derogatory: categories refer to “nonsense” in the sense that they are all about the “structure,” and not about the “meaning,” of what they represent.”

—Paolo Aluffi, Algebra: Chapter 0

Introduced in the 1940s by Mac Lane and Eilenberg in a paper titled “General Theory of Natural Equivalences”, Category theory is a branch of mathematics that aims to unify various mathematical structures from different fields and provide generalized concepts and theorems, and is thus known for its abstractness and generality. In this post, I am going to explain two important concepts in category theory: the Yoneda Lemma and the universal property. I am assuming that the reader understands introductory abstract algebra (knows the definition of groups, rings, fields, vector spaces and modules) and perferably some topology. Throughout this post, we accept the Zermelo-Fraenkel axioms as our foundations, and use \(\mathcal{U}\) to refer to the universe of all sets. However, it should also be noted that there are other books relating category theory where the universe \(\mathcal{U}\) in discussion is not the universe of all set but rather something else such as the Grothendieck Universe, which is not a topic of discussion in this post.

Definition 1. (Morphism) A morphism \(f\) from a class \(A\) to a class \(B\) is a triple \((A,B,S)\) where \(S\in\mathcal{U}\).

The class \(A\) is the source of the morphism and \(B\) is the target. We use the notation \(A \stackrel{f}{\longrightarrow} B\) to denote that \(f\) is a morphism from \(A\) to \(B\). A morphism is simply a class that contains all information about its source and target and possibly additional information. A function \(f:A\rightarrow B\) as a morphism from \(A\) to \(B\) is simply a morphism \((A,B,f)\). Let \(C\) be a class. For \(f^\prime:A\rightarrow B\) as a morphism \(f\) from \(A\) to \(B\) and \(g^\prime:B\rightarrow C\) as a morphism \(g\) from \(B\) to \(C\), we define their canonical composition \(g\circ f=(A,C,g^\prime f^\prime)\) where \(g^\prime f^\prime\) is the function composition of \(g^\prime\) and \(f^\prime\).

Definition 2. (Category) A category \(\mathcal{C}\) consists of a class \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) whose elements are called the objects, a mapping \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}\) with domain \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\times \mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) that maps \((A,B)\) to a class of morphisms from \(A\) to \(B\), and an operation \(\circ=\bigcup_{A,B,C\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})}\circ_{A,B,C}\) called the composition where
$$\circ_{A,B,C}:\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,C)\times \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B)\rightarrow \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,C)
$$for all \(A,B,C\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\), that satisfy the following two axioms,

  1. for all \(A,B,C,D\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) and for all \(f\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B),g\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,C),h\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(C,D)\)
    $$h\circ (g\circ f)=(h\circ g)\circ f$$
  2. for all \(X\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\), there exists \(1_X\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X,X)\) such that for all \(A,B\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) and \(f\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B)\)
    $$f\circ 1_A=1_B\circ f=f$$where we call \(1_X\) the identity morphism of \(X\).

Moreover, A category \(\mathcal{C}\) is locally small if \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B)\in \mathcal{U}\) for all \(A,B\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) and small if it is locally small and \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\in\mathcal{U}\). Let \(\mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{C}):=\mathrm{image}(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{c}})\) be the class of morphisms of the category \(\mathcal{C}\).

In instances where it does not cause confusion, we usually omit the subscripts in \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}\) and simply write \(\mathrm{Hom}\). I should emphasize that morphisms need not be functions as morphisms. In fact, in a way, a category is a multigraph with an operation on its edges set that satisfies something similar to the two axioms in the above definition. If \(A,B\) are finite sets, then there are only finitely many functions from \(A\) to \(B\) set-theoretically speaking, but there are infinitely many morphisms from \(A\) to \(B\). In addition, we should consider \(\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)\) and \(\mathrm{Hom}(C,D)\) disjoint unless \(A=C\) and \(B=D\).

To give you an idea, here are some examples of categories

  1. The category \(\mathbf{Set}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Set})\) is the class of all sets, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Set}}(A,B)\) is the set functions from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  2. The category \(\mathbf{Grp}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Grp})\) is the class of all groups, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Grp}}(A,B)\) is the set of group homomorphisms from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  3. The category \(\mathbf{Ring}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Ring})\) is the class of all rings, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ring}}(A,B)\) is the set of ring homomorphisms from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  4. The category \(\mathbf{Field}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Field})\) is the class of all fields, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Field}}(A,B)\) is the set of field homomorphisms from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  5. The category \(\mathbf{Vect}_{F}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Vect}_{F})\) is the class of vector spaces over the field \(F\), \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Vect}_{F}}(A,B)\) is the set of linear maps from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  6. The category \(\mathbf{Ban}_{1}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Ban}_{1})\) is the class of all Banach spaces, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ban}_{1}}(A,B)\) is the set of linear contractions from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  7. The category \(\mathbf{Top}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Top})\) is the class of all topological spaces, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Top}}(A,B)\) is the set of continuous mappings from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  8. The category \(\mathbf{Pos}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Pos})\) is the class of all posets, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Pos}}(A,B)\) is the set of order preserving mappings from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  9. The category \(\mathbf{Hask}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Hask})\) is the class of all Haskell types, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Hask}}(\texttt{A},\texttt{B})\) is the class of all Haskell functions (as morphisms) of type \(\texttt{A -> B}\), and the composition of two morphisms \(\texttt{f :: A -> B}\) and \(\texttt{g :: B -> C}\), where \(\mathtt{C}\) is another Haskell type, is the Haskell composition of functiioins \(\texttt{g . f}\).
  10. The category \(\mathbf{Cat}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Cat})\) is the class of all small categories, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Cat}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B})\) is the class of functors from \(\mathcal{A}\) to \(\mathcal{B}\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical functor composition.

For part 10, I mentioned functors between categories and functor compositions, which I haven’t defined yet. They will be dealt with later. For now, the most important thing is that you get the gist of what a category is. If you are familiar with abstract algebra, you will notice that many of these examples have similar types of morphisms. Recall, that a bijective homomorphism between groups, rings, or fields is an isomorphism, and a bijective continuous map between topologies that also has a continuous inverse is a homeomorphism. The terms “isomorphism” and “homeomorphism” is essentially the same thing when viewed from a higher angle: they are morphisms that preserve the structure and provide a one-to-one correspondance. Hence the definition,

Definition 3. A morphism \(f\in\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)\) in some category with objects \(A,B,X\) is

  1. a monomorphism if it is left-cancellable, that is,
    $$(f\circ g=f\circ h)\Longrightarrow (g=h)$$for all \(g,h\in\mathrm{Hom}(X,A)\).
  2. an epimorphism if it is right-cancellable, that is,
    $$(g\circ f=h\circ f)\Longrightarrow (g=h)$$for all \(g,h\in\mathrm{Hom}(B,X)\).
  3. a split monomorphism if it has a left inverse, that is, there exist \(g\in\mathrm{Hom}(B,A)\) with \(g\circ f=1_A\)
  4. a split epimorphism if it has a right inverse, that is, there exist \(g\in\mathrm{Hom}(B,A)\) with \(f\circ g=1_B\)
  5. a bimorphism if it is both a monomorphism and an epimorphism
  6. an isomorphism if it is both a split monomorphism and a split epimorphism.
  7. an endomorphism if \(A=B\).
  8. an automorphism if it is an isomorphism and an endomorphism.
