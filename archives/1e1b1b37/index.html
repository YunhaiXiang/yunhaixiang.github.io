Category Theory: An Introduction

Mar 4 2020 Mathematics 17 minutes read (About 2510 words)

“The language of categories is affectionately known as “abstract nonsense,” so named by Norman Steenrod. This term is essentially accurate and not necessarily derogatory: categories refer to “nonsense” in the sense that they are all about the “structure,” and not about the “meaning,” of what they represent.”

—Paolo Aluffi, Algebra: Chapter 0

Introduced in the 1940s by Mac Lane and Eilenberg in a paper titled “General Theory of Natural Equivalences”, Category theory is a branch of mathematics that aims to unify various mathematical structures from different fields and provide generalized concepts and theorems, and is thus known for its abstractness and generality. In this post, I am going to explain two important concepts in category theory: the Yoneda Lemma and the universal property. I am assuming that the reader understands introductory abstract algebra (knows the definition of groups, rings, fields, vector spaces and modules) and perferably some topology. Throughout this post, we accept the Zermelo-Fraenkel axioms as our foundations, and use \(\mathcal{U}\) to refer to the universe of all sets. However, it should also be noted that there are other books relating category theory where the universe \(\mathcal{U}\) in discussion is not the universe of all set but rather something else such as the Grothendieck Universe, which is not a topic of discussion in this post.

Definition 1. (Morphism) A morphism \(f\) from a class \(A\) to a class \(B\) is a triple \((A,B,S)\) where \(S\in\mathcal{U}\).

The class \(A\) is the source of the morphism and \(B\) is the target. We use the notation \(A \stackrel{f}{\longrightarrow} B\) to denote that \(f\) is a morphism from \(A\) to \(B\). A morphism is simply a class that contains all information about its source and target and possibly additional information. A set \(S\) as a morphism from \(A\) to \(B\) is the morphism \((A,B,S)\). Let \(C\) be a class. For function \(f^\prime:A\rightarrow B\) as a morphism \(f\) from \(A\) to \(B\) and function \(g^\prime:B\rightarrow C\) as a morphism \(g\) from \(B\) to \(C\), we define their canonical composition \(g\circ f=(A,C,g^\prime \circ f^\prime)\) where \(g^\prime \circ f^\prime\) is the function composition of \(g^\prime\) and \(f^\prime\).

Definition 2. (Category) A category \(\mathcal{C}\) consists of a class \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) whose elements are called the objects, a mapping \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}\) with domain \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\times \mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) that maps \((A,B)\) to a class of morphisms from \(A\) to \(B\), and an operation \(\circ=\bigcup_{A,B,C\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})}\circ_{A,B,C}\) called the composition where
$$\circ_{A,B,C}:\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,C)\times \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B)\rightarrow \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,C)
$$for all \(A,B,C\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\), that satisfy the following two axioms,

  1. for all \(A,B,C,D\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) and for all \(f\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B),g\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,C),h\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(C,D)\)
    $$h\circ (g\circ f)=(h\circ g)\circ f$$
  2. for all \(X\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\), there exists \(1_X\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X,X)\) such that for all \(A,B\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) and \(f\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B)\)
    $$f\circ 1_A=1_B\circ f=f$$where we call \(1_X\) the identity morphism of \(X\).

Moreover, A category \(\mathcal{C}\) is locally small if \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B)\in \mathcal{U}\) for all \(A,B\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) and small if it is locally small and \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\in\mathcal{U}\). Let \(\mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{C}):=\mathrm{image}(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{c}})\) be the class of morphisms of the category \(\mathcal{C}\).

In instances where it does not cause confusion, we usually omit the subscripts in \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}\) and simply write \(\mathrm{Hom}\). I should emphasize that morphisms need not be functions as morphisms. In fact, in a way, a category is a multigraph with an operation on its edges set that satisfies something similar to the two axioms in the above definition. If \(A,B\) are finite sets, then there are only finitely many functions from \(A\) to \(B\) set-theoretically speaking, but there are infinitely many morphisms from \(A\) to \(B\). In addition, we should consider \(\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)\) and \(\mathrm{Hom}(C,D)\) disjoint unless \(A=C\) and \(B=D\).

To give you an idea, here are some examples of categories

  1. The category \(\mathbf{Set}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Set})\) is the class of all sets, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\textbf{Set}}(A,B)\) is the set functions from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  2. The category \(\mathbf{Grp}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Grp})\) is the class of all groups, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Grp}}(A,B)\) is the set of group homomorphisms from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  3. The category \(\mathbf{Ring}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Ring})\) is the class of all rings, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ring}}(A,B)\) is the set of ring homomorphisms from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  4. The category \(\mathbf{Ban}_{1}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Ban}_{1})\) is the class of all Banach spaces, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ban}_{1}}(A,B)\) is the set of linear contractions from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  5. The category \(\mathbf{Top}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Top})\) is the class of all topological spaces, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Top}}(A,B)\) is the set of continuous mappings from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  6. The category \(\mathbf{Haus}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Haus})\) is the class of all Hausdorff topological spaces, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Haus}}(A,B)\) is the set of continuous mappings from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.
  7. The category \(\mathbf{Pos}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Pos})\) is the class of all posets, \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Pos}}(A,B)\) is the set of order preserving mappings from \(A\) to \(B\) as morphisms, and the composition \(\circ\) is the canonical composition.

Observe that in the above definitions, the terms “bijection” in sets, “isomorphism” in groups, rings and fields, “homeomorphism” in topologies, and “isometric bijective linear contraction” in Banach spaces are essentially the same thing when viewed from a higher angle: they are morphisms that preserve the structure and provide a one-to-one correspondance. Hence the definition,

Definition 3. A morphism \(f\in\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)\) in some category with objects \(A,B,X\) is

  1. an endomorphism if \(A=B\).
  2. a monomorphism if it is left-cancellable, i.e. \((f\circ g=f\circ h)\Longrightarrow (g=h)\) for all \(g,h\in\mathrm{Hom}(X,A)\).
  3. an epimorphism if it is right-cancellable, i.e. \((g\circ f=h\circ f)\Longrightarrow (g=h)\) for all \(g,h\in\mathrm{Hom}(B,X)\).
  4. a split monomorphism if it has a left inverse, i.e. there exist \(g\in\mathrm{Hom}(B,A)\) with \(g\circ f=1_A\)
  5. a split epimorphism if it has a right inverse, i.e. there exist \(g\in\mathrm{Hom}(B,A)\) with \(f\circ g=1_B\)
  6. a bimorphism if it is both a monomorphism and an epimorphism
  7. an isomorphism if it is both a split monomorphism and a split epimorphism.
  8. an automorphism if it is an isomorphism and an endomorphism.

Moreover, we use the notations \(\mathrm{End}_{\mathcal{C}}(X)\) and \(\mathrm{Aut}_{\mathcal{C}}(X)\) (or \(\mathrm{End}(X)\) and \(\mathrm{Aut}(X)\) if it does not cause confusion) to denote the class of endomorphisms and automorphisms on the object \(X\) of the category \(\mathcal{C}\), and we write \(A\cong B\) to denote that the objects \(A\) and \(B\) are isomorphic.

Monomorphisms and epimorphisms are generalizations of injections and surjections, and in fact the monomorphisms and epimorphisms in \(\mathbf{Set}\) are precisely the injections and surjections. It should be obvious to you that a split monomorphism is a monomorphism and a split epimorphism is an epimorphism, which means that an isomorphism is a bimorphism. However, a bimorphism is not necessaily an isomorphism. For example, the natural inclusion \(\mathbf{Z}\rightarrow\mathbf{Q}\) in the category \(\mathbf{Ring}\) is a bimorphism but not an isomorphism, and so are the natural inclusion \(\mathbf{Q}\rightarrow\mathbf{R}\) in the category \(\mathbf{Haus}\) and any non-identity morphism in the category \(\mathbf{Pos}\). Moreover, A small category where every morphism is an isomorphism is a groupoid. There is this joke that we can define a group as a groupoid with a single object, and it would make total sense mathematically, but “most mathematicians will find it ludicrous to introduce groups in this fasion, or they will at the very least politely express doubts on the pedagogical effectiveness of doing so”, as said by Paolo Aluffi.

Much like the morphisms in the previously mentioned categories, we can also define structure preserving maps between categories. In fact, the category \(\mathbf{Cat}\) is such that \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathbf{Cat})\) is the class of small categories, and for small categories \(\mathcal{A}\) and \(\mathcal{B}\), define \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\textbf{Cat}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B})\) as the class of functors from the category \(\mathcal{A}\) to the category \(\mathcal{B}\) as morphisms, where the notion of functors are defined as follows.

Definition 4. (Functors) Let \(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}\) be categories. A (covariant) functor \(\mathrm{F}\) from \(\mathcal{A}\) to \(\mathcal{B}\) is a pair of functions \((\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{obj}},\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{hom}})\) such that \(\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{obj}}:\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{A})\rightarrow \mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{B})\) and \(\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{hom}}:\mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{A})\rightarrow \mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{B})\) and for all \(X,Y,Z\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{A})\)

  1. \(\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{hom}}(1_X)=1_{\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{obj}}(X)}\)
  2. if \(f\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{A}}(X,Y)\) then \(\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{hom}}(f)\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{obj}}(X),\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{obj}}(Y))\)
  3. \(\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{hom}}(g\circ_{\mathcal{A}} f)=\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{hom}}(g)\circ_{\mathcal{B}} \mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{hom}}(f)\) for all \(f\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{A}}(X,Y)\) and \(g\in\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{A}}(Y,Z)\)

where \(\circ_{\mathcal{A}},\circ_{\mathcal{B}}\) represents the composition operation in the categories \(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}\) respectively. Moreover, the composition of two functors \(\mathrm{F}\circ \mathrm{G}\) is the pairwise composition of their object function and morphism function, i.e. \(\mathrm{F}\circ\mathrm{G}=(\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{obj}}\circ \mathrm{G}^{\mathrm{obj}},\mathrm{F}^{\mathrm{hom}}\circ \mathrm{G}^{\mathrm{hom}})\).

Before I provide some examples of functors, I should introduce a few new concepts. If \(\mathcal{C}\) is a category, then its opposite category is the category \(\mathcal{C}^{\textrm{op}}\) where \(\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C}^{\textrm{op}})=\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\) and \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}^{\textrm{op}}}(A,B)=\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,A)\). If \(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}\) are categories, a contravariant functor from \(\mathcal{A}\) to \(\mathcal{B}\) is a functor from \(\mathcal{A}^{\mathrm{op}}\) to \(\mathcal{B}\). A presheaf on a category \(\mathcal{C}\) is a contravariant functor from \(\mathcal{C}\) to \(\mathbf{Set}\). Finally, here are the examples,

  1. the identity functor \(1_{\mathcal{C}}\) from \(\mathcal{C}\) to \(\mathcal{C}\) with \(1_{\mathcal{C}}^{\textrm{obj}}:X\mapsto X\) and \(1_{\mathcal{C}}^{\textrm{hom}}:f\mapsto f\)
  2. the constant functor \(\Delta_{X}\) from \(\mathcal{A}\) to \(\mathcal{B}\) with \(\Delta_{X}^{\mathrm{obj}}:A\mapsto X\) and \(\Delta_{X}^{\mathrm{hom}}:f\mapsto 1_X\) where \(X\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{B})\)
  3. the functor \(\mathrm{F}\) from \(\mathbf{Grp}\) to \(\mathbf{Set}\) with \(\mathrm{F}^{\textrm{obj}}:(G,\cdot)\mapsto G\) and \(\mathrm{F}^{\textrm{hom}}:f\mapsto f\). Functors as such are called forgetful functors, since it forgets its operation. We can also construct forgetful functors from \(\mathbf{Ring}\) to \(\mathbf{Grp}\), and ones from \(\mathbf{Top}\) to \(\mathbf{Set}\), and so on. There is no precise definition for a forgetful functor
  4. the functor \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,-)\) from a locally small category \(\mathcal{C}\), where \(A\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\), to the category \(\mathbf{Set}\), such that the mappings \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,-)^{\mathrm{obj}}:X\mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,X)\) and \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,-)^{\mathrm{hom}}:f\mapsto (f\circ -)\) where \(f\circ -\) is the mapping \(g\mapsto f\circ g\) as a morphism. Similarly, we define a presheaf \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,B)\) of \(\mathcal{C}\) where \(B\in\mathrm{obj}(\mathcal{C})\), such that the mappings \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,B)^{\mathrm{obj}}:X\mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X,B)\) and \(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,B)^{\mathrm{hom}}:f\mapsto (-\circ f)\) where \(-\circ f\) is the mapping \(g\mapsto g\circ f\) as a morphism. These two functor are called the covariant and contravariant hom-functors. Moreover, any functor that is isomorphic to a hom-functor in \(\mathbf{Cat}\) is a representable functor.

Informally, we use the term “diagram” to refer to a multi-directed-graph, or a quiver, with vertices are objects and edges as morphisms to represent part of a category and a composition for edges. We use different notations for different kinds of morphisms: a hooked arrow \(\hookrightarrow\) to represent a monomorphism, and a double arrow \(\twoheadrightarrow\) for epimorphisms.
