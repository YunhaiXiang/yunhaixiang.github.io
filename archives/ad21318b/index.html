Quadratic Reciprocity Times Three

Feb 21 2019 Mathematics 3 minutes read (About 412 words)

Besides the Pythagorean theorem and the fundamental theorem of algebra, the law of Quadratic Reciprocity is probably the most oftenly proved theorem in math. In fact, on this website you can find 246 different proofs (collected by the mathematician Franz Lemmermeyer) of the theorem. In this article, I will discuss a few of them that I thought are the best ones. First, we define what a quadratic residue is.

Definition. For an odd prime $p$ and an integer $a\in\mathbf{Z}$ coprime to $p$, if there exists $n\in\mathbf{Z}$ such that $ a\equiv n^2\pmod{p}$ then we say that $a$ is a quadratic residue modulo $p$ (a quadratic non-residue otherwise).

It is easy to see that in $\mathbf{Z}/p\mathbf{Z}$ there are exactly $\frac{p-1}{2}$ quadratic residues, namely, $1^2,2^2,\dots,\left(\frac{p-1}{2}\right)^2$. To see this, note that if $i^2\equiv j^2\pmod{p}$ for $1\le i,j\le \frac{p-1}{2}$, then $p\mid (i+j)(i-j)$ but $2\le i+j\le p-1$ so $i\equiv j\pmod{p}$. Next we introduce the Legendre symbol, with which we can write the law of quadratic reciprocity concisely.

Definition. For an odd prime $p$ and $a\in \mathbf{Z}$
$$
\left(\dfrac{a}{p}\right):=
\left\{\begin{matrix}
1&a\mathrm{\; is\; a\; quadratic\; residue\; modulo\;}p\\
-1&a\mathrm{\; is\; a\; quadratic\; nonresidue\; modulo\;}p\\
0&a\equiv 0\pmod{p}\\
\end{matrix}\right.
$$
Theorem (Quadratic Reciprocity). If $p,q$ are two distinct odd primes, then
$$
\left(\dfrac{p}{q}\right)\left(\dfrac{q}{p}\right)=(-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}\cdot \frac{q-1}{2}}
$$
To prove this, we first introduce a useful lemma by Euler. By Fermat’s little theorem, in $(\mathbf{Z}/p\mathbf{Z})[x]$, the polynomial $x^{p-1}-1=(x^{\frac{p-1}{2}}+1)(x^{\frac{p-1}{2}}-1)$ takes on roots over all nonzero residues. And, since if $a\equiv n^2\pmod{p}$ is a quadratic residue, then $a^{\frac{p-1}{2}}\equiv n^{p-1}\equiv 1\pmod{p}$, the quadratic residues are precisely roots of ​$x^{\frac{p-1}{2}}-1$, in other words,

Lemma (Euler’s Criterion). For an odd prime $p$ and $a\in \mathbf{Z}$
$$
\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)=a^{\frac{p-1}{2}}\pmod{p}
$$
This immediately gives the corollary that the Legendre symbol is a completely multiplicative function, that is $\left(\frac{ab}{p}\right)=\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)\left(\frac{b}{p}\right)$ for $a,b\in\mathbf{Z}$ and odd prime $p$.
