Milking the Integrals!

Jul 10 2019 Mathematics 5 minutes read (About 820 words)

The phrase “milking the integral” was coined by the MSE user Frpzzd, who defines it as “obscure, rare, or asthetically pleasing integral tricks” that makes use of clever substitutions, symmetry of the integrand, differentiating under the integral sign, expanding the integrand as series, and other devilishly sly maneuvers. This post is dedicated to collecting and demonstrating some of these esoteric integral tricks, much of which comes from Paul Nahin’s book Inside Interesting Integrals, and others from MSE, AoPS, PUMaC, HMMT, SMT, etc. For a quick appetizer, try evaluate
$$
\int_0^\pi\ln(a^2-2a\cos x+1)\,\mathrm d x
$$where \(a>0\). We will see later in this post that this integral can be evaluated in at least five different ways, which is simply astounding!

Exploiting the Symmetry of the Integrand

In many cases, mathematics tends to reward you when you respect its symmetry, and when it comes to milking integrals, sometimes we can exploit the hidden symmetries in the integrand and solve the integral surprisingly quickly. Case in point, try the following example.

Example 1. Evaluate
$$\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{\cos x}{e^{\sin^3 x}+1}\,\mathrm dx$$

Hint

Observe that the interval of integration is symmetric, but the integrand is not an odd function. Also note that \(\cos x\) is even and \(\sin^3 x\) is odd. Try the substitution \(u=-x\) and see if you can simplify the integrand by getting rid of the denominator.

Solution

By symmetry or the substitution \(u=-x\), we can easily obtain
$$I=-\int_{\pi/4}^{-\pi/4}\frac{\cos (-u)}{e^{-\sin^3 u}+1}\,\mathrm du=\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{\cos x}{e^{-\sin^3 x}+1}\,\mathrm dx$$Note that \(u\) is merely a dummy variable, so we can change it to \(x\). Therefore,
$$
\begin{aligned}
2I&=\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\left(\frac{\cos x}{e^{\sin^3 x}+1}+\frac{\cos x}{e^{-\sin^3 x}+1}\right)\,\mathrm dx\\
&=\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\left(\frac{\cos x}{e^{\sin^3 x}+1}+\frac{e^{\sin^3x}\cos x}{1+e^{\sin^{3}x}}\right)\,\mathrm dx\\
&=\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{(1+e^{\sin^3x})\cos x}{1+e^{\sin^3x}}\,\mathrm dx\\
&=\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\cos x\,\mathrm dx\\
&=\sqrt{2}
\end{aligned}
$$Therefore \(I=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\).

Generalizing the technique we have the following theorem.

Theorem 1. For \(a,b\in\mathbf{R}\) and \(f\) integrable on \([a,b]\)
$$
\int_a^bf(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=\int_a^bf(a+b-x)\,\mathrm dx=\frac{1}{2}\int_{a}^b\left(f(x)+f(a+b-x)\right)\,\mathrm dx
$$

Excercise 1. (Applications of Theorem 1)

  1. Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/4}\ln(1+\tan x)\,\mathrm dx\)
  2. Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{\sin x+\cos x}{1+\sqrt[5]{\tan x}}\,\mathrm dx\)
  3. Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_3^7\frac{\ln (x+2)}{\ln (-x^2+10x+24)}\,\mathrm dx\)
  4. Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_{-\pi}^\pi\frac{x^2}{ 1+\sin x+\sqrt{1+\sin^2x}}\,\mathrm dx\)

However, this is not the only form of symmetry we can exploit. Observe, that for a improperly integrable function \(f\) over \((0,\infty)\), the integral \(\displaystyle\int_0^\infty f\), when using the substitution \(u=1/x\) gives us another integral over \((0,\infty)\). Using this property, we can milk some integrals whose integrands have hidden symmetries with respect to inversion. For example,

Example 2. Evaluate
$$
\int_0^\infty\frac{\ln \frac{1+x^{11}}{1+x^3}}{(1+x^2)\ln x}\,\mathrm dx
$$

Hint

There are two ways one can go about solving this integral. You can either directly apply the substitution \(u=1/x\), or you can first use the fact that \(\displaystyle\int_0^\infty f=\int_0^1 f+\int_1^\infty f\) then use the substitution \(u=1/x\) on the second integral.

Solution

First we have
$$
\int_0^\infty\frac{\ln \frac{1+x^{11}}{1+x^3}}{(1+x^2)\ln x}\,\mathrm dx=\int_0^1\frac{\ln \frac{1+x^{11}}{1+x^3}}{(1+x^2)\ln x}\,\mathrm dx+\int_1^\infty\frac{\ln \frac{1+x^{11}}{1+x^3}}{(1+x^2)\ln x}\,\mathrm dx
$$By the substitution \(u=1/x\),
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_1^\infty\frac{\ln \frac{1+x^{11}}{1+x^3}}{(1+x^2)\ln x}\,\mathrm dx&=-\int_1^0\frac{\ln \frac{1+u^{-11}}{1+u^{-3}}}{u^2(1+u^{-2})\ln u^{-1}}\,\mathrm du\\
&=-\int_0^1\frac{\ln u^{-8}\frac{1+u^{11}}{1+u^{3}}}{(u^2+1)\ln u}\,\mathrm du\\
&=-\int_0^1\frac{-8\ln u+\ln\frac{1+u^{11}}{1+u^{3}}}{(u^2+1)\ln u}\,\mathrm du\\
&=8\int_0^1\frac{1}{u^2+1}\,\mathrm du-\int_0^1\frac{\ln \frac{1+x^{11}}{1+x^3}}{(1+x^2)\ln x}\,\mathrm dx
\end{aligned}
$$Therefore,
$$
\int_0^\infty\frac{\ln \frac{1+x^{11}}{1+x^3}}{(1+x^2)\ln x}\,\mathrm dx=8\int_0^1\frac{1}{u^2+1}\,\mathrm du=2\pi
$$

Theorem 2. For improperly integrable function \(f\) over \((0,\infty)\),
$$
\int_0^\infty f(x)\,\mathrm dx=\int_0^\infty\frac{1}{x^2}f\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\,\mathrm dx=\int_0^1\left(f(x)+\frac{1}{x^2}f\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right)\,\mathrm dx$$

Excercise 2. (Applications of Theorem 2.)

  1. Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \frac{\ln x}{1+x^2}\,\mathrm dx\)
  2. Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^\infty\frac{\mathrm dx}{(x+1)^2(x^\pi+1)}\,\mathrm dx\)
