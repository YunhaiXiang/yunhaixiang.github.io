#Group Theory

Burnside's Lemma and Polya Enumeration Theorem

Jul 11 2019 Mathematics 5 minutes read (About 737 words)

Burnside’s lemma is a lemma of elementary group theory that is very useful in counting, and often seen as an “overkill” trick in math competitions. Intuitively, Burnside’s lemma counts the number of arrangements or permutations of an object if some of its symmetries are considered identical. For example,

Example 1.

How many distinct colorings can be obtained with \(6\) different colors to color the faces of a cube? Two configurations are considered identical if one can be obtained from the other through rotation.

Solution

There are \(30\) distinct colorings. Consider the group of rotations of a cube \(G\) acting on \(\mathrm{S}_6\), there are \(3\times 3=9\) rotations about opposite faces, \(6\times 1 = 6\) rotations about opposite edges, and \(4\times 2=8\) rotations about opposite vertices. Together with the identity rotation we have \(|G|=9+6+8+1=24\) (in fact, \(G\) is isomorphic to \(\mathrm{S}_4 \)). For \(g=e\), we have \(X^g=X\), and for \(g\ne e\), we have \(X^g=\varnothing\) since all colors on the faces are different. Hence the number of orbits is
$$
\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g\in G}|X^g|=\frac{|X|}{|G|}=\frac{6!}{24}=30
$$which means that there are \(30\) distinct colorings.

To approach such problem, we start with the definitions of orbits, stabilizers, and fixed points in group actions.

Definition 1.

Let \(G\) be a group that acts on \(X\), the orbit of a point \( x\in X\) is defined as \( G\cdot x=\{g\cdot x\mid g\in G\} \), the stabilizer of a point \( x\in X\) is defined as \( G_x =\{g\in G\mid g\cdot x=x\} \), and the fixed points of \(g\in G\) in \(X\) is the set \( X^g=\{x\in X\mid g\cdot x=x\} \).

Note that for \(x,y\in X\), either the orbits \(G\cdot x=G\cdot y\) or \((G\cdot x)\cap (G\cdot y)=\varnothing \). This is an easy result since if \(a\in (G\cdot x)\cap (G\cdot y)\) then \(a=g_1\cdot x=g_2\cdot y\), for some \(g_1,g_2\in G\), so \(x=g_1^{-1}g_2\cdot y\) and \(y=g_2^{-1}g_1\cdot x\), hence \((G\cdot x)\subseteq (G\cdot y)\) and \((G\cdot y)\subseteq (G\cdot x)\). Also, consider the map \(\varphi:G/G_x\rightarrow G\cdot x\) defined by \(\varphi(gG_x)=g\cdot x\). We can easily see that \(\varphi\) is an isomorphism, which gives us \(|G\cdot x|=\left[G:G_x\right]\), also known as the orbit-stabilizer theorem.

Theorem 1 (Burnside’s Lemma).

Consider a group \(G\) acting on a set \(X\), then the number of distinct orbits is
$$
\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g\in G}|X^g|$$

Proof

Let \(S=\{(g,x)\in G\times X\mid g\cdot x= x\}\), we have
$$
|S|=\sum_{g\in G}|X^g|
$$Let \(B\) be the set of orbits, and by the orbit-stabilizer theorem,
$$
|S|=\sum_{x\in X}|G_x|=|G|\sum_{x\in X}\frac{1}{|G\cdot x|}=|G|\sum_{A\in B}\sum_{a\in A}\frac{1}{|A|}=|G|\sum_{A\in B}1=|G||B|
$$And we are done.

Excercise 1. (Easy)

  1. How many colorings are there to color the four edges of a square using \(n\) colors? Two configurations are considered identical if one can be obtained from the other through reflections and rotations.
  2. (USAMTS 2008) \(27\) cubes with \(25\) black cubes and \(2\) white cubes are assembled to form a \(3\times 3\times 3\) large cube. How many identical large cubes can be formed?
  3. (AIME II 2010) How many polynomials \(f\in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbf{Z})\) with integer zeros is such that \(f(0)=2010\)?

Excercise 2. (Moderate)

  1. A \(k\)-ary necklace of length \(n\) is a string of \(n\) beads each one of \(k\) colors, where rotations are considered equivalent. Find the number of \(k\)-ary necklaces of length \(p\) where \(p\) is prime, and prove that the number of different \(k\)-ary necklaces of length \(n\) is
    $$
    N_k(n)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{d\mid n}k^d\varphi\left(\frac{n}{d}\right)$$
  2. A \(k\)-ary bracelet of length \(n\) is a string of \(n\) beads each one of \(k\) colors, where rotations and reflections are considered equivalent. Prove that the number of different \(k\)-ary bracelet of length \(n\) is
    $$
    B_k(n)=\left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{1}{2}N_k(n)+\frac{1}{4}(k+1)k^{\frac{n}{2}} & \mathrm{if} &k\equiv 0\pmod{2}\\\frac{1}{2}N_k(n)+\frac{1}{2}k^{\frac{n+1}{2}}& \mathrm{if}& k\equiv 1\pmod{2}\end{matrix}\right.
    $$
