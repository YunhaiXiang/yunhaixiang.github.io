#Linear Algebra

The Smith Determinant

Jul 12 2019 Mathematics a minute read (About 213 words)

Here’s a curious identity I ran across on AoPS: the determinant of an \(n\times n\) matrix where the \((i,j)\) entries is \(\gcd(i,j)\) happends to be the product of the Euler totient values from \(1\) to \(n\).

Theorem (Smith’s Determinant).
$$
\det\begin{pmatrix}
\gcd(1,1)&\gcd(1,2)&\cdots&\gcd(1,n)\\
\gcd(2,1)&\gcd(2,2)&\cdots&\gcd(2,n)\\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots &\vdots\\
\gcd(n,1)&\gcd(n,2)&\cdots&\gcd(n,n)
\end{pmatrix}=\prod_{m=1}^n\varphi(m)
$$where \(\varphi(\cdot)\) is the Euler totient function.

The proof of this result is surprisingly simple: to describe it briefly, multiply the first row by \(-1\) and add it to the \(2,3,4,…,n\) rows, multiply the second row by \(-1\) and add it to the \(2,4,6,…\) rows, multiply the third row by \(-1\) and add it to the \(3,6,9,…\) rows, and so on. Then use of the identity \(\sum_{d\mid n}\varphi(d)=n\) to simplify. Another method is to use of UD decomposition, decomposing the original matrix into
$$
\begin{pmatrix}
\varphi(1)&0&0&0&0&\cdots\\
\varphi(1)&\varphi(2)&0&0&0&\cdots\\
\varphi(1)&0&\varphi(3)&0&0&\cdots\\
\varphi(1)&\varphi(2)&0&\varphi(4)&0&\cdots\\
\varphi(1)&0&0&0&\varphi(5)&\cdots\\
\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots\\
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
1&1&1&1&1&\cdots\\
0&1&0&1&0&\cdots\\
0&0&1&0&0&\cdots\\
0&0&0&1&0&\cdots\\
0&0&0&0&1&\cdots\\
\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots\\
\end{pmatrix}
$$And hence by the multiplicative property of determinants, we have our desired result.
